Hands-on Descriptive Analytics with Excel

This section outlines the practical exercise designed to give you direct experience in performing descriptive analytics using Microsoft Excel. You will work with a sales dataset to create various charts and summarize your findings, reinforcing the concepts learned in N-S11.

Your Tasks:

You will be provided with a sales dataset in Excel. Your goal is to apply basic Excel functions and charting capabilities to answer specific descriptive business questions and then summarize your observations.

Task 1: Overall Sales Performance

  • Calculate the `Total Sales Revenue` for the entire dataset.
  • Calculate the `Average Sales Amount per Transaction`.
  • Identify the `Maximum` and `Minimum` `Sales Amount` in a single transaction.

Expected Outcome: A 1-2 sentence summary of these overall figures, providing a quick snapshot of the business's scale and transaction value range.

Task 2: Sales Trend Over Time

  • Create a **Line Graph** showing `Total Sales Revenue` per `Month` (or `Date`, if daily data is available).

Expected Outcome: A 2-3 sentence summary describing the trend observed in the line graph. Look for patterns like seasonality, growth, or decline (e.g., "Sales show an increasing trend with a peak in December, indicating seasonality").

Task 3: Sales by Product Category

  • Create a **Bar Chart** showing `Total Sales Revenue` for each `Product Category`.

Expected Outcome: A 2-3 sentence summary highlighting the top-performing and lowest-performing product categories. This helps identify which products contribute most to revenue.

Task 4: Sales Distribution by Region

  • Create a **Pie Chart** (or another Bar Chart, if preferred, recalling the discussion on pie chart limitations from N-S11) showing the `Percentage of Total Sales` contributed by each `Region`.

Expected Outcome: A 1-2 sentence summary about regional sales distribution, indicating which regions are most dominant in terms of sales.

Key Learning: The goal of this exercise is not just to create charts, but to *understand what the charts are telling you* about the business. Focus on interpreting the visual insights and translating them into concise summaries.

Predictive Analytics: Purpose, Techniques & Data Requirements

Having solidified our understanding of "what happened" with descriptive analytics, we now shift our focus to "what *could* happen." Predictive analytics uses historical data, statistical models, and machine learning techniques to forecast future outcomes or probabilities. The goal is to anticipate, rather than just react.

  • Forecasting Future Outcomes: Estimating future values (e.g., sales, stock prices, customer demand).
  • Identifying Future Risks & Opportunities: Predicting potential problems (e.g., customer churn, equipment failure, fraud) or emerging trends.
  • Guiding Proactive Strategies: Enabling businesses to take action *before* events occur.
  • Optimizing Resource Allocation: Allocating resources more effectively based on anticipated needs.
  • Personalization: Tailoring offers and experiences based on predicted individual behavior.
  • 1. Forecasting (for continuous values):

    Concept: Predicting a future numerical value, often used for time-series data.

    Simple Example: Predicting next month's sales revenue based on past sales trends.

    Techniques (Conceptual): Time Series Models (analyzing trends, seasonality), Regression Analysis (predicting based on relationships between variables).

  • 2. Classification (for categorical outcomes):

    Concept: Predicting which category or class an item belongs to (the outcome is a discrete label).

    Simple Example: Predicting whether a customer will "churn" (Yes/No) or "not churn."

    Techniques (Conceptual): Logistic Regression (for binary classification), Decision Trees (flowchart-like structure), k-Nearest Neighbors (classifies based on nearest data points), Naive Bayes (probabilistic classifier, often for text).

  • 3. Clustering (Unsupervised - briefly for context):

    Concept: Grouping similar data points together without predefined categories. While primarily unsupervised, the *results* of clustering can inform predictive models (e.g., predicting behavior for a newly identified customer segment).

    Simple Example: Grouping customers into distinct segments based on their purchasing behavior.

  • Historical Data (Labeled Data for Supervised Learning): Paramount. Models learn from past examples where the outcome is known.
  • Features (Independent Variables): Input variables the model uses to make predictions (e.g., customer demographics, past purchase frequency for churn prediction).
  • Target Variable (Dependent Variable): The variable you are trying to predict (e.g., a binary variable for churned/not churned).
  • Sufficient Quantity: Predictive models often require large amounts of data to learn robust patterns.
  • High Quality: Data must be clean, consistent, and accurate. Errors or missing values lead to flawed predictions.
  • Relevance & Timeliness: Historical data must be relevant to the current context and recent enough to capture current trends.

Connection to Prescriptive Analytics (Teaser):

Predictive analytics tells us *what will happen*. Prescriptive analytics then takes that prediction and tells us *what to do about it*. For example, if a predictive model says a customer is likely to churn, a prescriptive system might recommend a specific retention offer. This will be explored in N-S13 and N-S14.
