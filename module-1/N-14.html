Overview: Prescriptive Analytics & Connecting the Types

Welcome to this interactive guide for N-S14, where we explore **Prescriptive Analytics** – the most advanced form of data analytics. Having understood what happened (Descriptive) and what could happen (Predictive), we now focus on **"What should we do?"** Prescriptive analytics moves beyond insights and forecasts to provide concrete, actionable recommendations, guiding businesses towards optimal decisions.

This guide will challenge you to classify prescriptive questions, then walk you through compelling real-world scenarios like airline pricing and retail promotions, demonstrating how Descriptive, Predictive, and Prescriptive analytics work together in a powerful, sequential flow. Understanding this interconnectedness is key to unlocking the full potential of data-driven decision-making. Use the navigation above to explore each topic in detail.

Scenario Analysis: Airline Pricing - The Sequential Flow

Let's explore how a major airline, 'SkyHigh Airlines,' uses data analytics to manage its flight ticket pricing (dynamic pricing or revenue management) by sequentially applying Descriptive, Predictive, and Prescriptive analytics.

1. Descriptive Analytics: "What Happened?" (Understanding Past Performance)

Question: "What has been our historical booking behavior and revenue for Flight SH101 (e.g., from New York to London)?"

  • Data: Historical booking data (number of seats sold, booking date, price paid, passenger class, cancellation rates), historical flight occupancy rates, revenue per flight.
  • Insights:
    • "Flight SH101 typically sells out 80% of its economy seats 3 days before departure."
    • "Average revenue per seat for business class on SH101 is $1,500."
    • "Demand for SH101 is highest on Tuesdays and Fridays."
    • "Cancellation rates are higher for bookings made more than 60 days in advance."
  • Tools: Dashboards, reports, basic charts (line graphs for booking trends, bar charts for revenue by class).

2. Predictive Analytics: "What Could Happen?" (Forecasting Future Demand)

Question: "What is the likely demand for Flight SH101 next month, and what is the probability of it selling out at current prices?"

  • Data: Historical descriptive data, current booking trends for future flights, competitor pricing, holiday schedules, major events in destination cities, fuel prices, macroeconomic indicators.
  • Insights:
    • "Based on current trends and upcoming holidays, Flight SH101 is predicted to be 95% full next month."
    • "There is a 70% probability that all business class seats will be sold out 7 days before departure."
    • "Demand for economy seats is predicted to soften slightly due to a new competitor route."
  • Tools: Statistical models (e.g., time series forecasting, regression), machine learning algorithms (e.g., classification models for predicting sell-out probability).

3. Prescriptive Analytics: "What Should We Do?" (Recommending Optimal Actions)

Question: "Given our predicted demand and current bookings, what is the *optimal price* for each remaining seat on Flight SH101 to maximize revenue?"

  • Data: All descriptive and predictive insights, current seat availability, competitor prices, historical price elasticity of demand, revenue targets, operational costs.
  • Insights/Recommendations:
    • "Increase the price of the remaining 10 business class seats by 15% immediately, as they are predicted to sell out."
    • "Offer a 5% discount on economy seats booked within the next 48 hours to stimulate demand, as a competitor has introduced a new route."
    • "Consider offering premium economy upgrades at a reduced rate to passengers who booked economy more than 30 days ago, to fill those seats."
  • Tools: Complex optimization algorithms (e.g., linear programming, dynamic programming), simulation models, recommendation engines. These systems often automate price changes in real-time.

Key Takeaway: This scenario clearly shows how understanding the past (descriptive) informs predictions about the future (predictive), which then drives specific, optimized actions (prescriptive), creating a powerful, integrated system for complex decision-making.

Unifying Example: Optimizing Retail Promotions

Let's consider another example: a retail chain, "StyleMart," wants to optimize its weekly promotional offers to maximize sales and customer engagement, while also managing inventory effectively.

1. Descriptive Analytics: "What Happened?" (Understanding Past Promotions)

Purpose: To understand how past promotions performed and what the current state of sales and inventory is.

  • Questions: "What were the total sales and profit margins for each product category during last month's 'Summer Sale'?", "Which promotional offers generated the highest revenue?", "What is the current inventory level for our top 10 best-selling items?"
  • Insights: StyleMart learns that "20% Off" promotions on denim historically drive the highest sales volume, but "Buy One Get One Free" on accessories yields higher profit margins. They also see that their current inventory of popular t-shirts is running low.

2. Predictive Analytics: "What Could Happen?" (Forecasting Future Demand and Behavior)

Purpose: To forecast future demand for products and predict how customers will respond to different types of promotions.

  • Questions: "What is the forecasted demand for denim and accessories next quarter?", "Which customer segments are most likely to respond positively to a 'Buy One Get One Free' offer?", "What is the probability that our popular t-shirts will go out of stock next week?"
  • Insights: The predictive model forecasts strong demand for accessories next quarter, especially among younger demographics. It also predicts that a "Buy One Get One Free" offer will likely lead to a 30% increase in accessory sales among loyalty members. The model also warns that t-shirt stock will deplete in 5 days.

3. Prescriptive Analytics: "What Should We Do?" (Recommending Optimal Promotional Strategy)

Purpose: To recommend the optimal promotional strategy for StyleMart to maximize sales and profit, considering inventory constraints and customer segments.

  • Questions: "Given the predicted demand and customer responsiveness, what specific promotional offers should StyleMart run next week on which products, and to which customer segments, to maximize overall revenue?", "What is the optimal reorder quantity for the popular t-shirts?"
  • Recommendations: "Launch a 'Buy One Get One Free' offer on accessories, targeted specifically via email to loyalty program members, starting this Friday." "Initiate a reorder for 5,000 units of the popular t-shirts immediately, selecting the express shipping option." "Run a '15% Off All Denim' promotion on social media and paid ads, targeting new potential customers, starting Monday."

Impact: StyleMart implements these data-driven recommendations, leading to increased sales, optimized inventory, higher customer engagement, and ultimately, improved profitability. This example highlights the value hierarchy: Descriptive provides the *foundation*, Predictive offers *foresight*, and Prescriptive delivers *actionable intelligence*.

Key Takeaways: The Value and Interconnectedness

The journey through Descriptive, Predictive, and Prescriptive analytics reveals a clear progression in complexity and value. Each type builds upon the last, creating a powerful, integrated system for data-driven decision-making.

The Value Hierarchy:

  • Descriptive Analytics: Answers "What happened?" Provides a foundational understanding of past events and current states. Essential for monitoring and reporting.
  • Predictive Analytics: Answers "What could happen?" Uses historical data to forecast future outcomes or probabilities. Enables proactive strategies and risk assessment.
  • Prescriptive Analytics: Answers "What should we do?" Not only predicts but also suggests optimal actions to achieve desired outcomes. Drives automation, optimization, and superior business results.

The Iterative Nature:

It's important to remember that the data analytics lifecycle is highly iterative. The results of prescriptive actions (e.g., sales from new promotions) become new descriptive data, which then feeds back into the predictive models, allowing for continuous learning and refinement of future strategies. This creates a powerful, self-improving cycle of data-driven decision-making.

Ultimately, a robust data analytics capability leverages all three types in synergy, providing a holistic view and actionable intelligence that allows businesses to not just understand and predict, but to actively shape their future.
