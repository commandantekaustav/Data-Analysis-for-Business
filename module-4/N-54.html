1. Introduction to the T-Distribution

Before diving into t-tests, it's important to understand the **t-distribution** (also known as Student's t-distribution). This distribution is similar to the normal distribution but has fatter tails, especially for smaller sample sizes. This accounts for the increased uncertainty when estimating population parameters from small samples.

  • Degrees of Freedom (df): The shape of the t-distribution is determined by its degrees of freedom. For a single sample, $df = n - 1$, where $n$ is the sample size. As $df$ increases (i.e., as sample size increases), the t-distribution approaches the standard normal (Z) distribution.
  • When to use t-distribution instead of Z-distribution:
    • When the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is unknown and must be estimated from the sample standard deviation ($s$).
    • When the sample size ($n$) is small (typically $n < 30$). For larger samples, the t-distribution becomes very similar to the Z-distribution, and either can be used, though t-tests are generally preferred when $\sigma$ is unknown.

Comparison of T-distributions with different degrees of freedom and the Standard Normal (Z) distribution. Notice how t-distributions have fatter tails for smaller df.

2. One-Sample T-test

The one-sample t-test is used to determine whether a sample mean ($\bar{x}$) is significantly different from a hypothesized population mean ($\mu_0$).

  • Hypotheses:
    • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The sample mean is equal to the hypothesized population mean.
      $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$
    • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_a$): The sample mean is different from, greater than, or less than the hypothesized population mean.
      $H_a: \mu \ne \mu_0$ (Two-tailed)
      $H_a: \mu > \mu_0$ (Right-tailed)
      $H_a: \mu < \mu_0$ (Left-tailed)
  • Formula for the t-statistic: $$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}$$ Where:
    • $\bar{x}$ = sample mean
    • $\mu_0$ = hypothesized population mean
    • $s$ = sample standard deviation
    • $n$ = sample size
  • Assumptions:
    • The sample is randomly selected from the population.
    • The data are continuous (interval or ratio scale).
    • The population from which the sample is drawn is approximately normally distributed. (This assumption becomes less critical with larger sample sizes due to the CLT).
  • Applications:
    • Testing if the average weight of products from a new machine is still 500g.
    • Determining if the average customer satisfaction score has changed from a benchmark of 7.

3. Two-Sample T-test (Independent Samples)

The independent samples t-test (also known as Student's t-test or unpaired t-test) is used to compare the means of two independent groups to determine if there is a statistically significant difference between them.

  • Hypotheses:
    • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The means of the two populations are equal.
      $H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$ (or $H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$)
    • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_a$): The means of the two populations are different, or one is greater/less than the other.
      $H_a: \mu_1 \ne \mu_2$ (Two-tailed)
      $H_a: \mu_1 > \mu_2$ (Right-tailed)
      $H_a: \mu_1 < \mu_2$ (Left-tailed)
  • Formula for the t-statistic (assuming equal variances - pooled variance): $$t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2)}{s_p \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}}}$$ Where $s_p$ (pooled standard deviation) is: $$s_p = \sqrt{\frac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}}$$ And:
    • $\bar{x}_1, \bar{x}_2$ = sample means of group 1 and group 2
    • $s_1^2, s_2^2$ = sample variances of group 1 and group 2
    • $n_1, n_2$ = sample sizes of group 1 and group 2
    *(Note: There is also a formula for unequal variances, often called Welch's t-test, which adjusts the degrees of freedom. Most software automatically handles this.)*
  • Assumptions:
    • The two samples are independent.
    • The data are continuous (interval or ratio scale).
    • The populations from which the samples are drawn are approximately normally distributed. (Less critical with larger sample sizes).
    • **Homogeneity of Variances:** The variances of the two populations are approximately equal (for the standard Student's t-test). If not, Welch's t-test is more appropriate.
  • Applications:
    • Comparing the effectiveness of two different teaching methods on student test scores.
    • Determining if a new drug significantly lowers blood pressure compared to a placebo.
    • Assessing if there's a difference in average sales between two different store layouts.

4. Paired Samples T-test (Brief Mention)

While not the primary focus, it's important to know about the paired samples t-test. This test is used when the two samples are related or dependent (e.g., before-and-after measurements on the same individuals, or matched pairs).

  • It analyzes the differences between paired observations, effectively reducing variability and increasing statistical power for detecting an effect.
  • Example: Comparing blood pressure before and after a treatment on the *same* patients.

Choosing the correct t-test is crucial for valid statistical inference. Always consider your research question, data structure, and assumptions before applying a test.
